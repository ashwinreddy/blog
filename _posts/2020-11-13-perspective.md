---
title: "The Hunt For Perspective"
---

Today I upgraded my laptop to the latest macOS. I don't particularly care about the new features in the operating system; I just like getting updates out of the way quickly, so I can get bug fixes and not have to deal with the persistent reminders.

As is often the case with upgrades, my laptop stopped working as it normally should. This time, the issue was that my internet refused to connect. Even though a good chunk of my time as a computer science major is spent debugging issues, I found myself vexed by the problem in front of me which I most certainly did not ask for.

While I was solving the issue, I felt a combination of anger at the problem, sadness for an inability to solve it immediately, and dread at the thought of how I would be productive without a laptop on a Zoom-era Friday.

Then I recalled the ideas I had read a few weeks ago about stoicism and the Bhagavad Gita. These are two philosophies that tell you to stop focusing on the results of your actions and the instinctive emotions that come from everyday problems. All that's under our control is the next step in the course of action.

As I was making a little headway with the issue, I texted my father. Not because I thought he would know how to solve the problem off the top of his head. The problem is essentially mine to solve.

Debugging is fundamentally about two coupled virtues -- patience and perspective. When you note a discrepancy between what is expected and what is observed, it becomes clear that you are lacking some perspective. There is something you don't yet know. And if the solution were simple, it likely would have presented itself already. Therefore, one needs the patience to systematically pinpoint the issue.

If your goal is to build the virtues of patience and perspective, then pair programming is debauchery. With two people, each is constantly getting a second perspective. Each mind can track an idea and explain it to the other; thus, less patience is required.

So I asked because I suspected the perspective would help. As they say, two heads are better than one. And when he saw the text and I explained the issue, it was clear to both him and me exactly what to do to fix it.

Lately I've been thinking about what values I hold. We often hear words like integrity, courage, or kindness when we talk about values. I think perspective should be near the top of the list. First, there was the perspective I obtained from reading about stoicism and the Bhagavad Gita. It helped keep me centered while solving an obnoxious problem. Second, there was the perspective of my father, who helped me determine what the issue likely was.


Other people often have interesting ways of seeing the world. To ask someone for advice is to borrow their perspective, to temporarily use some of their brain cycles on a problem that you're having. Of course, not all perspective is useful; we have to separate the wheat from the chaff. But it's amazing what we can start to comprehend when we try to understand how others think.
