---
title: "Leveraging Anecdotal Evidence"
---

Anecdotal evidence typically does not hold up in scientific discussions, many forms of debate, and other formal settings. Interlocuters will see its use as a logical fallacy. While it is true that a single person's experiences should not be grounds for determining, say, national public policy, in everyday circumstances people find anecdotal evidence entirely acceptable as a means of gaining information. This post describes why in some cases reasoning with ancedotal evidence is an approximation to the best possible answer.

First, let's define our terms.

**Anecdotal evidence**: using the experience of yourself or people you know as the basis for a belief or action.

**Scientific experiment**: collecting samples of data under controlled conditions so that statistical inferences are meaningful (i.e. unlikely to be merely anomalous).

For the purposes of this post, use examples from biology or sociology when thinking of an experiment. People don't usually rely on anecdotal evidence for their fundamental assumptions about physics and chemistry, and even if they did, there's not much barrier to trying out a small-scale experiment to verify the key axioms.

In any case, observe that anecdotal evidence and scientific experiment are not unalike. The difference lies in two factors: rigor and scale. Rigor refers to how reliable the experiment results are, probably based on the number of controlled variables. Scale refers to the size of population the experiment samples from.

||Small scale|Large scale|
|Low Rigor|**Pure anecdote**|Stereotype|
|High Rigor|Scientific anecdote?|**Scientific experiment**|

Let's illustrate these with the example of finding a diet:

* If you simply take a friend's story about her personal diet choices and assume they are right for you, you are using pure anecdote.
* If you carefully record what you are eating every day and note differences in your energy levels, you are using well-documented experience to find the right diet. I'll call this a scientific anecdote, but I'm sure a better term exists or can be created.
* If you are just listening to a lot of people who subscribe to _X_ dietary method, you probably have a stereotype of how that diet actually affects them.
* If you read studies about how _X_ diet affects people, you are using scientific experiments.

We can start by dismissing the stereotypes out of hand, leaving 3 modes of inquiry: anecdotes, experience, and experiments. If you want to have understanding of a large population (large scale), experiments trump the other two.

If, however, the population you care about is small, for example just includes you and your family, the experiment is irrelevant. You do not want inferences about the larger population. Then, documenting personal experience in as detailed a manner as you can is the best option; anecdotal evidence is an extremely weak and relaxed version of this mode of inquiry.

Students run into this problem when they look at grade distributions. Suppose the grade is normally distributed around a B+ average. What does this mean for you, in particular? Well, unless you're exactly average in this class, this number is kind of meaningless.  Asking your friends how they did in the class gives you a more accurate estimate since you and your friends are likely to have a similar work ethic and/or interest in the subject and/or general intelligence. This is using anecdotal evidence, but it's more reasonable than the science experiment that is the overall grade distribution.

Similarly, no matter how much one reads about different diets, the diet you should have is the one that empirically works the best for you. Studies can suggest the approach, but the results obviously vary from person to person. As a study increases in specificities particular to someone, they should weight it more highly. I would probably pay a lot of attention to any studies that talk about diet for an Indian male in his early 20's compared to one studying American citizens more broadly.


Next time you ask a friend how they got that job that you want, realize that you're gaining information by using anecdotal evidence; your implicit assumption is that the steps and difficulties they faced in getting that job will be similar to the ones you will face. Anecdotal evidence is perfectly valid _conditioned on_ having a small scale that the information applies to.
