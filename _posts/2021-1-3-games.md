Chapter 3 of Bernard Suits' _The Grasshopper_ (1978) arrives at a Suitable definition for **game** from first principles.[^witt]

[^witt]: Primarily written for other philosophers, the book responds to Ludwig Wittgenstein's contention that there's nothing that can define games concretely save the vague resemblances we see between games. 

First, why do we associate games with fun, amusement, and whimsy? We might locate this amusement in the fact that games force us to accomplish goals in roundabout ways. 

Consider the racetrack below. If the goal was simply to go from START to END, why waste time going around the track when you can take SHORTCUT through the grass? The only reason is because the rules of the game demand it.

<img src="https://imgur.com/0bA8o4C.png" style="width: 400px;" />

Games create **inefficiency** in this way. Let $G$ be some game of interest. The goal of $G$ we'll denote as $\Omega(G)$ since $\Omega$ is the last letter in the Greek alphabet, and the goal marks the end of the game. The argument is that if you only wanted to achieve $\Omega(G)$, there are more efficient ways of doing it than playing $G$. The rules only make getting to the end goal harder.[^eff]

Suits calls $\Omega(G)$ the **prelusory goal** of $G$. _Pre_ means before and _ludo_ means "I play" in Latin, suggesting that you can describe the prelusory goal without reference to the game mechanics. To do so, describe the prelusory goal as a _specific state of affairs_. For example, chess checkmates consist in certain configurations of the board, the race ends with someone crossing the finish line, etc.

[^eff]: The inefficiency is with respect to the means that are allowed in getting to the end goal. Good players are _as efficient as possible_ subject to the inefficiencies created by the rules. The runners in the track above will run as fast as they can, given that they have to run in an arc.

### Following Rules!?

Why would we possibly want to follow rules that make getting to the prelusory goal harder, you ask? Purely so that we can play a game! The rules distinguish the game from an ordinary activity. If you aren't following the rules, you're not playing the game. If we also consider what it means to win a game, we conclude the following, where $A$ is a player in a game $G$:
\begin{equation}
A \text{ won } G \implies A \text{ played } G \implies A \text{ followed the rules of } G.
\end{equation}
You can't win a game without playing it, and if you played it, you must have followed the rules.

As it stands, our definition is too broad. Let's repurpose the race track figure above as a metaphor for a different game. 

* START is you being in the local Philosophy Club as a regular member.
* END is you being the president of the club. 
* TRACK would be running for office the honest way. 
* SHORTCUT is assassinating the current president.

For the sake of argument, let SHORTCUT be a more expedient path than TRACK. If you decide to run for office for the reason that assassination is morally and legally reprehensible, you are not playing a game even though you are taking a less efficient path towards an end goal. The efficient option is basically not an option at all because laws and morals are **binding rules**, which means they get applied universally in our lives.

The qualification we introduce to solve this problem is to say the rules must be ultimately non-binding: you must feel you have the ability, at any time, to stop playing. In sum,

> Playing a game is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles.

### An Alternate Definition

This Wizards of the Coast [article](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/making-magic/what-game-2018-06-04) also has a compelling definition for game, which shares many similarities to the Grasshopper one.

> A game is a thing with a goal (or goals), restrictions, agency, and a lack of real-world relevance.

* The goal(s) refer to the prelusory goal.
* The restrictions are the inefficiency-generating rules. Without these, we just have an activity.
* The agency is implicit in the phrase "attempt to overcome" in Suits' definition. Without this, we just have an event.
* The lack of real-world relevance is the ultimately-non-binding nature of the rules. Without this, we have Life Itself.


### Explaining Games

In my experience, people tend to be pretty awful at explaining games to new players. "You'll pick it up as you watch" is pragmatic advice (and it can be fun to deduce the rules of a new game), but our new definitions give us a principled way of explaining games.

1. Explain the prelusory goal since, in principle, this can always be done without reference to the rules. Let the player know what they're aiming for.
2. Explain the primary means by which they can inch towards the prelusory goal.
3. Explain any secondary game mechanics or omit them for the time being if too complex.
4. Explain basic strategy of the game (i.e. why the inefficiencies are interesting).

### So What?

According to the Wizards of the Coast article, relaxing the condition on relevance gives us a definition for life, suggesting life is a kind of generalized game or that games are mini-versions of life.

Is game-playing at the core of human experience? [^utopia] Certainly the world imposes rules (physical, societal, etc.) that limit us from getting what we want, but we decide to overcome those obstacles anyway. 

[^utopia]: Suits thinks that in a utopia, people would only play games. 

Suppose games are at the core of life. Since all games are fundamentally similar in structure and form, if we learn how to play one game well, we might then be in a better position to pick up other games as well. If we repeat this process of learning different games, we could become excellent at the game of learning-how-to-play-games. Two general strategies then emerge:

* Either learn how to play one game so well that you outclass competitors, or
* Learn the ability to pick up games quickly and find a niche for yourself.

This philosophy of games might thus be connected to the existential idea that people get to set up their own goals. We'll leave the work of figuring out the interplay between goal-setting and game-playing for another day.
