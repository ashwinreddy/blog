I first learned about the hero's journey in fifth grade English class, when we used it to understand the story structure of _Kung Fu Panda_ (2008). Now, ten years later, I have finally read the book that conceived it in its modern form, Joseph Campbell's _The Hero With a Thousand Faces_ (1949).

# Big Ideas

Campbell is fundamentally interested in the commonalities between mythological stories. Clearly, myths form a genre of their own, with their own stock characters, plot devices, etc. But Campbell's claim is more interesting than that. At the risk of oversimplifying, he says that all popular stories share the same mythological structure, which we can call the hero's journey or the **monomyth**. 

Ultimately, we need to hear the same story in different forms because the symbols lose their meaning over time. Thus, they must be updated in form and language from era to era. What matters is not the particular character or symbol, but the spiritual concept to which it points. In particular, the monomyth advocates for people to emulate the hero, a person that consciously undergoes a difficult and dangerous journey and transforms in the process. 

Campbell presents this journey or adventure as a cycle consisting of some 15 steps (read the book if you want details and examples for each step). Folk tales tend to revolve around just one or two of the steps while epics like the Odyssey consist of many full adventure cycles stitched together. Dan Harmon, the creator of _Community_ and _Rick and Morty_, has boiled the hero's journey into an even simpler structure called the story circle. I will present a hybrid between the two that highlights the salient and interesting points.

**The Hero**

We start with our hero, the protagonist. They may be poor or rich, already told they are destined for greatness or not, but the important details are that this hero is in a zone of comfort, living in a status quo, and needing or desiring something whether they know it or not. [This article](https://bookpub.club/post/what-makes-a-character-memorable-1597349751016x998449421677232100) describes some of the kinds of desires they might have. By the end of the cycle, they will have become something greater, having achieved a state of "self-achieved submission."

_Examples_: Harry Potter lives with the Dursleys, Po works at a noodle shop, Luke Skywalker lives on Tatooine.

**Crosses a threshold**

The hero goes on a journey or quest, spurred on by themselves or by an external force. They may refuse the call, at first, but they eventually decide to go. This is step is harder than it seems:

> The usual person is more than content, he is even proud, to remain within the indicated bounds, and popular belief gives him every reason to fear so much as the first step into the unexplored.

If this external force is a person, that person may bestow them with special tools for the adventure. 

_Examples_: Perseus goes to slay Medusa; Athena lends him a sword, Hermes winged sandals, and Hades a helm of darkness.

**Walks the Road of Trials**

The hero has left the place of comfort, and is now in a space of "curiously fluid, ambiguous forms." They encounter creatures never seen before and discover that they are not what they thought in this labyrinth. In the fight, they are transformed.

> The hero, whether god or goddess, man or woman, the figure in a myth or the dreamer of a dream, discovers and assimilates his opposite (his own unsuspected self) either by swallowing it or by being swallowed. One by one the resistances are broken. He must put aside his pride, his virtue, beauty, and life, and bow or submit to the absolutely intolerable. Then he finds that he and his opposite are not of differing species, but one flesh. 

_Examples_: Dante travels through each circle of Hell, Theseus fights the Minotaur in the labyrinth.

**Faces the Greatest Challenge / Earns a Reward**

The hero's reward is peculiar to his own circumstances, but Campbell outlines the many ways this step may unfold:

> The triumph may be represented as the hero's sexual union with the goddess-mother of the world (sacred marriage), his recognition by the father-creator (father atonement), his own divinization (apotheosis), or again -- if the powers have remained unfriendly to him -- his theft of the boon he came to gain.

In essence, the hero by the end of this step will have learned to detach himself from the delusions of ordinary life and the blind spots he had when he began the journey. 

_Examples_: Po fights Tai Lung, Luke shoots the Death Star.

**The Return**

The hero must return to where they started, with knowledge of how they have evolved. They may be rescued or resurrected in the process. They relate their teachings to society.

_Examples_: The resurrection of Christ, Simba becomes the new king.

## Short Review of Book

I thoroughly enjoyed reading the first half of the book, which closely analyzes each step of the hero's journey. Each chapter covers the basic idea of that step and then describes its psychological meaning as well as myriad examples from myths and stories from across the globe. The stories are engaging, and the rest of the chapter generalizes the basic tenet seen in the story.

The second half has less structure since it doesn't have the neat structure of a cycle to hang onto. For that reason, I became less engaged because the points being made were increasingly abstract and didn't further deepen my understanding of the hero's journey.

Overall, I really enjoyed this book and the writing style. My only complaint is that Campbell often describes an ordinary person's dream and then analyzes it to connect it to the step of the hero's journey he is discussing. I don't find this kind of evidence convincing; on the contrary, it feels contrived compare to the potent myths which he has at hand.
